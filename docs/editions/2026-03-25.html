OpenAI Kills Sora, Pivots Team to Robotics — Disney's $1B Deal Dies With It

OpenAI said it's closing Sora, the short-form video app that went viral after its launch six months ago. With the shutdown of the Sora app, ChatGPT also will no longer generate video based on text prompts. Disney has now ended its partnership with OpenAI, which included plans for the media conglomerate to take a $1 billion stake in the artificial-intelligence company. The economics were brutal: running Sora at scale was extremely expensive — a November 2025 report from Forbes estimated that OpenAI could be spending as much as $15 million per day generating AI videos. The strategic pivot is the real story. A spokesperson said the Sora research team "continues to focus on world simulation research to advance robotics that will help people solve real-world, physical tasks." Sam Altman also laid out a reorg, moving safety under Mark Chen's research org and security under Greg Brockman's scaling org. He said OpenAI has finished pretraining its next model, codenamed Spud, which he expects to produce a "very strong model" in a few weeks.

Why it matters → ** OpenAI is explicitly rerouting its most compute-hungry team from entertainment to physical-world simulation for robotics. This is one of the clearest signals yet that the company sees embodied AI — not consumer media — as the next frontier worth burning GPUs for. The closure comes ahead of an expected IPO, and by shifting compute away from Sora, OpenAI could reallocate chips to more lucrative coding, reasoning, or text-generation tasks.

Policy

Sanders and AOC Introduce Data Center Moratorium Bill

Bernie Sanders (I-Vt.) and Rep. Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez (D-N.Y.) on Wednesday announced legislation to pause all new data center construction nationwide until AI safeguards are in place. The proposed Artificial Intelligence Data Center Moratorium Act would pause all new AI data center construction nationwide, and the moratorium would remain in place until federal legislation establishes protections for workers, consumers, privacy, civil rights, and the environment. Data center projects could potentially be on hold for years under the Sanders-AOC bill, as Congress is far from passing any AI legislation. Nearly 3,000 data centers are under construction or planned across the U.S., with 4,000 already in operation.

Why it matters → ** The bill almost certainly won't pass in a Republican-controlled Congress, but it marks a new front in the AI backlash — one focused not on models or algorithms, but on the physical infrastructure they depend on. In Frederick County, a grassroots campaign gathered over 22,000 signatures for a referendum on data center zoning. Community-level opposition to data centers is becoming a real constraint on buildout timelines.

MARKET

** If enacted, this would freeze trillions in planned capex from MSFT, GOOG, META, and AMZN. Utilities and data center REITs like Equinix (EQIX) and Digital Realty (DLR) would face acute risk. In practice, the bill signals political headwinds that could slow permitting even without passage. Watch for state-level copycats. ---

The Bigger Picture

Today's news draws a remarkably clean picture of where the power centers in AI are consolidating. The same executives spending $300B+ annually on AI infrastructure now formally advise the president on its regulation — a level of regulatory capture that would have been unthinkable five years ago but feels almost inevitable given the speed at which this industry moves. Meanwhile, the populist counter-response has arrived not as a ban on models or algorithms, but as a moratorium on the buildings that house them — a much more visceral, local-politics-friendly angle that could gain traction at the state and county level even if it fails in Congress. OpenAI's decision to shutter its flashiest consumer product to redirect compute toward robotics and world simulation is perhaps the most telling signal of the week: the company that once dreamed of becoming the AI-era TikTok now sees its future in physical-world agents. Pair that with Unitree shipping 5,500 profitable humanoid robots and heading for a $6B public listing, and the "embodied AI" narrative is no longer speculative — it's generating auditable revenue. The question for investors isn't whether robots will enter factories and eventually homes, but which vertical in the stack — chips, actuators, world models, or complete systems — captures the most durable margin. ---
